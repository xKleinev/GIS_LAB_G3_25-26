Data & Methods

What we used, where it came from, and how the eight processing steps turn raw atmospheric and demographic data into the maps and figures on the Results page.

Study Area

This study focuses on Metropolitan France (including Corsica) as its primary area of interest (AOI). The core objective of this project is to evaluate the spatial and statistical correlations between land surface dynamics and atmospheric pollution trends over a three-year study timeframe (2021-2023). Specifically, the analysis is structured around three main requirements:

  • Quantifying Land Cover Dynamics: Utilizing high-resolution imagery to isolate and analyze multi-temporal land cover transitions-specifically mapping surface alterations such as urban expansion, forest conservation, and agricultural stability.
  • Assessing Atmospheric Concentration Shifts: Monitoring annual mean aggregation changes for three critical target criteria pollutants: Nitrogen Dioxide (NO2), Fine Particulate Matter (PM2.5), and Coarse Particulate Matter (PM10).
  • Evaluating Dynamic Population Exposure: Overlaying high-resolution demographic datasets with reclassified pollution layers to model human health risks across regional administrative corridors.

Processing Pipeline

CAMS reanalysis NetCDF data was projected to WGS84 (EPSG:4326). To manage the high-dimensionality of the hourly data, we aggregated time-series to monthly means, followed by a yearly average to produce annual concentration surfaces.

Annual means were reclassified into 5 distinct concentration classes using the thresholds defined by WHO (2021) guidelines. We utilized the Reclassify by Table tool in the Processing Toolbox to apply the specific breakpoint ranges for NO2, PM2.5, and PM10, ensuring each map effectively highlights zones exceeding safe exposure levels.

To quantify atmospheric evolution, we applied the AMAC formula: (Mean_2023 - Mean_2021) using the Raster Calculator. This generated a diverging change detection surface. The symbology was standardized using a blue–white–red diverging .qml style, where blue represents concentration decrease and red signals hotspots of pollution increase.

Transitions were computed via the unique ID formula: (LC_2021 * 100) + LC_2023. Technical Decision: Due to performance constraints during vector Dissolve operations at 10m, we downsampled the Land Cover to a 100m resolution. This enabled reliable matrix generation, highlighting major urban expansion and deforestation patterns across France.

Using the Zonal Statistics algorithm, we calculated the mean, minimum, and maximum pollutant changes within our land cover transition zones. The result was vectorized to facilitate clear bar chart visualization, demonstrating the mitigation effect of tree cover persistence on local particulate matter levels.

We integrated population density (classified into 5 quantiles) and pollutant classes into a 3x3/5x5 grid. The bivariate attribute was calculated using: (pol_class_max * 10) + pop_class_median. This map visually emphasizes regions where high population density overlaps with the highest pollution concentration tiers.

Final exposure counts were derived by dissolving the bivariate layer to group by pollution class and summing population raster values via Zonal Statistics. This transformation resulted in a compact dataset (max 5 features per pollutant), representing the percentage share of the national demographic exposed to specific concentration levels.

Choices & Challenges

What we decided, and what got in the way

Key Choices

  • CAMS Reanalysis over Ground Sensors: We prioritized CAMS data for its comprehensive spatial coverage across France, as ground-based station networks provide only point-specific data that is insufficient for national-level bivariate mapping.
  • Clip-First Strategy: We mandated clipping all raw raster layers to the GAUL Level 2 administrative AOI at the start of the pipeline to optimize RAM usage during geoprocessing.
  • Standardized Resampling: By choosing to align all layers to a 100m grid, we ensured that population, pollution, and land cover pixels were perfectly spatially coincident for our zonal statistic calculations.

Problems Encountered

Technical Challenges & Data Alignment Decisions

During the processing phase of Lab 3, our group encountered significant performance bottlenecks when attempting to perform geometric Dissolve operations on high-resolution Land Cover raster datasets (10m). The computational overhead of processing multi-temporal transitions at 10m resolution exceeded available local system memory, leading to recurring process timeouts.

To resolve this, we performed a downsampling of the Land Cover data to a 100m resolution framework.

  • Why this decision? By aligning the high-resolution Land Cover data to the coarser pollutant grid, we dramatically reduced the dataset dimensionality.
  • Scientific Impact: This alignment ensures that our cross-variable correlation analysis (Step 6) is performed on topologically identical grid cells, preventing spatial mismatch errors and significantly improving the reliability of our zonal statistics computations.
